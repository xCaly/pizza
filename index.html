Rodel Tapaya

One of the most active and successful artists in Southeast Asia.

Rodel Tapaya is a leading Philippine painter who is known for his large-scale canvases, narrative murals and sculptural installations that evoke the primordial rainforests of indigenous folklore as allegories for the present reality. His works have gained renown and critical acclaim by winning in several regional art contests and exposure in international exhibitions. Tapaya's work is his ongoing amalgamation of folk narrative and contemporary reality within the framework of memory and history.

Tapaya creates unique cosmologies out of labyrinthine forms, weaving together mythical creatures with hallucinogenic landscapes that recall the magic realism and political praxis of Mexican muralists. In doing so, the artist addresses the conflict between tradition and rapid urbanisation, global labour and rural exodus, and the nation's ongoing postcolonial traumas. By interpreting them through a new artistic lens, he creates an interaction between popular culture and ancient myth.

NOTABLE ACHIEVEMENTS

  1. As a student Tapaya was a semi-finalist of the Metrobank Young Painters' Annual Art Competition.

  2. Tapaya also scored first place at the Shell National Students Art Competition in the Watercolor Category.

  3. In honor of his achievements, the President of the Republic of the Philippines, Benigno Simeon Aquino III, presented him with an Ani ng Dangal Award at the Malacanang Palace.

  4. In 2001 he won the Nokia Art Awards competed among young artists in the Southeast Asian region.

  5. Rodel Tapaya was also shortlisted four times to the Ateneo Art Awards in four separate years and won a jury prize to the Phillip-Morris Philippine Art Awards in 2007.

  6. “Ang Batang Maraming Bawal” is the winner of The Center for Art, New Ventures and Sustainable Development's (CANVAS') second annual Romeo Forbes Children's Storywriting Competition and Rodel Tapaya was selected by the publisher to illustrate the book before the story was selected. A number of writer's submitted their stories based on a single painting by Tapaya.
